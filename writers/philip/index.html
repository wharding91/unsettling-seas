M. NourbeSe Philip (born 3 February 1947) is a Tobagonian-born Canadian poet, novelist, essayist, and dramatist. Born in Woodlands, Moriah, Tobago, she was educated at the University of the West Indies and the University of Western Ontario, where she studied law and political science. She practiced law in Toronto for seven years before leaving in 1983 to write full-time. Her work is characterized by formal experimentation in the service of social justice, and she has held writer-in-residence positions at universities across Canada and the United States.

Philip’s bibliography on this site gathers creative works from the 1989–2001 period. The selected works listed here foreground poetry and prose represented in the local BibTeX files for the project.

Literary Significance

Philip’s She Tries Her Tongue; Her Silence Softly Breaks is a landmark text in postcolonial and experimental literature, recognized for dismantling the colonial English language itself as a site of violence and recovery. She is one of the foremost Caribbean-Canadian writers and a foundational figure in Black diaspora poetics.
